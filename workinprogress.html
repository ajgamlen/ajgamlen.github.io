Work in Progress

The Story

When I set out to create Work in Progress, it began as a project focused on the visual identity of a single band. But as I delved deeper, the project transformed into something much more personal—a reflection of how craft, creativity, and collaboration intersect in our lives.

Drawing inspiration from the messy, iterative nature of creativity, this publication is not just a finished product but a glimpse into the process itself.

At its core, Work in Progress seeks to:

  • Celebrate the Interplay Between Work and Play: Exploring the blurred boundaries between labor and leisure, the project values experimentation, process, and imperfection as integral parts of creativity.

  • Highlight How Craft Shapes Cultural Narratives: Through tactile design and material choices, Work in Progress underscores the role of craft in expressing identity and challenging polished design norms.

  • Explore the Creative Processes Behind Design and Music: The publication reveals the sparks, iterations, and collaborations that bring multidisciplinary work to life.

  • Strengthen Connections Across Creative Disciplines: By sharing diverse perspectives, the project invites readers to find resonance and universality in artistic expression.

  • Encourage Reflection on the ‘Messy Middle’: Celebrating the unfinished and exploratory stages of creativity, it highlights the value of discovery in the process.

Behind the Scenes

1. Research

To ground this project, I interviewed four creatives: Fin Pinkerton, Pearl, Guy Yarrall, and Freya Pinkerton, whose artistic practices span music, visual art, and storytelling. Their perspectives shaped the publication’s content and design.

2. Design Iteration

The tactile nature of the publication came from embracing imperfection. Pages were photocopied, reprinted, and marked intentionally, giving it a raw, working-document aesthetic.

3. Material Choices

Each material was chosen to evoke a sense of tactility and authenticity: Zeta paper for the body, tracing and butter papers for accents, and craft card for the cover. The Japanese stab binding method left the spine exposed, reinforcing the handmade quality.

Expressive Typography

Typography serves as visual breaks throughout the publication. For example, "Sound & Vision" mirrors waveforms, while "Towards Collective Creativity" resembles a winding road. These designs embody the themes they represent.

Artist Profiles

Each profile highlights an artist's unique journey: Fin Pinkerton’s exploration of fractal textures. Pearl’s narrative approach to design. Guy’s insights on collaboration within his band, Shooless. Freya’s storytelling through music and visual art.
