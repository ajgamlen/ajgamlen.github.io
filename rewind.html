2023, The Legacy of Video Stores

Be Kind, Rewind

In this day and age of ubiquitous digital streaming and fast access to an endless range of films, the very concept of a video store might conjure up strong associations with a different time. These physical locations, which used to be teeming centres of movie discovery, are now just vacant lots that reverberate with echoes of the past. The emptiness of video stores has a way of bringing us all back to a simpler time, when picking out a film was an experience laden with anticipation, discovery, and connection.

The sight of a video store triggers a wave of memories, transporting us to the days when Friday nights meant family outings to browse the aisles in search of the perfect film. The vacant shelves that once held rows of DVDs or VHS tapes now symbolise a lost era of tangible media consumption. Each empty slot seems to whisper the titles of movies that were once watched and cherished, a reminder of the stories that captured our imaginations.

These spaces are more than physical stores; they are repositories of personal experiences. The emptiness becomes a canvas onto which memories are projected, as recollections of childhood and family traditions intertwine with the architecture of the store. An empty video store evokes a bittersweet nostalgia due to the sense of loss it encapsulates. It represents the end of an era where the communal act of selecting a movie involved conversations, recommendations, and the tactile experience of holding a physical copy. The emptiness serves as a tangible reminder of how technology has reshaped our interactions with media, leaving behind a void where human connections once thrived.

A video store is a time capsule, a testament to the technological shift from analogue to digital. The juxtaposition of an empty store with memories of cover art and the anticipation of a movie night transports us to a time when films were not just consumed but experienced. It prompts reflections on the technological transformations that have led to convenience but have also diluted the tactile charm that was once an integral part of movie-watching.

These spaces offer a unique blend of past and present, a place where retro aesthetics and modern realities converge. The emptiness of video stores symbolises the transition from a physical world to a digital one, leaving us nostalgic for a time when movie rentals were not transactions but adventures in their own right. 

Yet, the emptiness also opens the door to fresh beginnings. It sparks discussions about the evolution of entertainment, from browsing shelves to scrolling through digital catalogues. The memories of empty video stores can serve as a reminder of the ever-changing landscape, inviting us to appreciate the convenience of streaming while cherishing the moments that once defined our movie-watching experiences.

Amanda Gamlen, 2023
